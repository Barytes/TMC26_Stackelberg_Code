# SPEC.md

## Objective

Design the experiment section so that it directly validates the paper's theoretical and algorithmic contributions under the **VBBR-BRD route**, with **Stage I as the main focus** and **Stage II only providing the necessary follower-side oracle evidence**.

The experimental narrative should not be a generic benchmark suite. It should answer the following question:

> Does the proposed two-stage framework work for the reasons claimed by the theory and the new solver design?

In particular:

- **Stage II** should only show that Algorithms 1 and 2 form a reliable and scalable **SOE approximation oracle** for follower response evaluation.
- **Stage I** should be the main experimental focus, validating the usefulness of upper-boundary / boundary-price geometry, the necessity of verified gain evaluation, and the efficiency/equilibrium quality of the proposed **Verified Boundary Best-Response Dynamics (VBBR-BRD)** route.
- **Strategic-setting experiments** should verify the economic conclusions of the model rather than merely reporting raw numbers.
- **Ablation experiments** should isolate the structural ingredients that make VBBR-BRD work in practice, especially the outer-policy design, the acceptance rule, and the mechanisms used to reduce Stage-II calls.

---

## Core Claims to Validate

### Stage II: follower-side oracle

The experiments should validate only the following claim:

- **Algorithms 1 and 2 are a sufficiently good SOE approximation oracle**: their social cost is close to the centralized optimum, and their runtime / communication cost scales well with the number of users.

In addition, the experiment design should explicitly confirm that the Stage-II output is not merely a centrally imposed solution, but is also **low-exploitability from the users' perspective**.

---

### Stage I: main contribution

The experiments should validate the following claims:

1. **Upper-boundary / boundary-price geometry is useful**  
   For a fixed candidate offloading set, good unilateral price proposals are concentrated at induced upper-boundary / boundary-event prices. Therefore, the Stage-I solver does not need to search the entire 2D price plane blindly.

2. **Verified evaluation is necessary**  
   Closed-form surrogate scores are useful for ranking candidate proposals, but they cannot replace true follower-response evaluation. The Stage-I solver should therefore rely on **verified unilateral gain**, i.e., candidate prices re-evaluated through the Stage-II solver.

3. **The VBBR proposal mechanism is effective**  
   The boundary-induced candidate proposals generated by the VBBR oracle should frequently contain high-quality unilateral deviations, so that the oracle can reduce verified gap without resorting to grid search.

4. **Outer-policy design is essential**  
   The order in which providers update (e.g., ESP-first, NSP-first, EN, NE, safe cyclic), as well as the acceptance rule, strongly influences whether the search trajectory enters and stays in the low-gap valley.

5. **Best-so-far and monotone acceptance are essential near equilibrium**  
   The main difficulty near equilibrium is not only entering the low-gap region, but also avoiding subsequent updates that leave it. Thus, incumbent tracking and monotone acceptance should materially improve final verified gap.

6. **VBBR-BRD reaches low verified $\epsilon$ efficiently**  
   The proposed solver should produce small verified deviation gaps while using far fewer expensive Stage-II calls than generic black-box baselines.

7. **Stage-II call reduction mechanisms matter**  
   Lazy verification, singleton-first candidate generation, caching, one-provider-per-round evaluation, periodic full-$\epsilon$ audits, and coarse-to-fine refinement should materially reduce Stage-II calls without large loss in final quality.

8. **VBBR-BRD is better suited to this problem than generic baselines**  
   PBRD, BO, DRL, and GSO may all be applicable, but they do not exploit the upper-boundary geometry, boundary-event prices, or verified candidate proposals. Under comparable budgets, they should be slower, use more Stage-II calls, and/or return larger final verified deviation gaps.

---

### Strategic-setting conclusions

The experiments should validate the following conclusions:

1. **Provider-side selfish pricing matters**  
   Selfish pricing competition between ESP and NSP should yield significant utility gains over non-strategic provider models such as Market Equilibrium.

2. **Centralized provider coordination remains stronger on provider utility**  
   The full strategic model may still produce lower provider utility than a centralized pricing/control benchmark such as SingleSP.

3. **User-side selfish offloading matters**  
   Competitive user-side offloading should reduce user social cost compared with non-strategic user-side baselines such as SingleSP and Random Offloading.

4. **Provider-side non-coordination hurts users**  
   User social cost should still be worse than in Market Equilibrium because providers do not coordinate.

5. **Complementary-resource effects become more visible under resource asymmetry**  
   The differences among strategic settings should be more pronounced when the ratio $F/B$ varies.

## Implementation Notes

- **Stage II should remain an oracle-side module**, not the main experimental story. The purpose of Stage-II experiments is to justify using Algorithms 1 and 2 as the follower-side solver inside Stage I.
- **Stage I should be reported primarily in terms of verified metrics**, not surrogate metrics. In particular, the main convergence and final-quality plots should use verified unilateral gains and verified $\epsilon$.
- **Small-scale instances** should be used for:
  - heatmap visualization,
  - trajectory overlays,
  - GSO comparison,
  - proposal-quality diagnostics,
  - exact or near-exact sanity checks where feasible.
- **Medium- and large-scale instances** should be used for:
  - runtime,
  - Stage-II call counts,
  - final verified $\epsilon$,
  - strategic-setting comparisons.
- **Common metrics to log** should include:
  - runtime,
  - total Stage-II calls,
  - Stage-II call breakdown,
  - current verified $\epsilon$,
  - best-so-far verified $\epsilon$,
  - accepted updates / attempted updates,
  - final user social cost,
  - final ESP revenue,
  - final NSP revenue,
  - final realized offloading-set size.

## Recommended Main-Text Storyline

A clean main-text narrative should be:

1. **Stage II oracle quality and scalability** (Figures 1--4)  
   Show that Algorithms 1 and 2 are accurate and scalable enough to serve as the follower-side oracle.

2. **Stage I verified convergence and geometry** (Figures 5--6)  
   Show that VBBR-BRD reduces verified gap and that the trajectory is guided toward a narrow low-gap region in price space.

3. **Why the VBBR oracle works** (Figure 7)  
   Show that the surrogate proposal mechanism is useful for ranking which boundary-event prices are worth verifying.

4. **Why outer-policy design matters** (Figure 8)  
   Show that update order and acceptance rules strongly affect whether the trajectory reaches and stays near equilibrium.

5. **Practical cost and scalability** (Figure 9 + Figure 14)  
   Show where the computational cost comes from and which mechanisms reduce it.

6. **Economic conclusions of the full model** (Figures 10--13)  
   Show that the full strategic model produces a distinct user/provider tradeoff and reveals meaningful complementary-resource effects.

7. **Near-equilibrium robustness** (Figure 15 + Appendix Figure A3)  
   Show that the crucial issue near equilibrium is not only entering the low-gap valley, but also retaining low-gap solutions.
